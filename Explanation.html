Understanding the Annual Cash Flows Chart

The Annual Cash Flows chart gives you a year-by-year picture of all the money coming in and going out during your retirement plan. It answers the question: "Where is my money coming from, and where is it going?"

What the Chart Shows

The chart is a stacked bar chart with two halves:

  • Above the zero line — all income (inflows) for each year, shown in shades of green.
  • Below the zero line — all expenses (outflows) for each year, shown in shades of red.
  • The black line — your net cash flow (income minus expenses). When this line is above zero, you have surplus cash that year. When it dips below zero, you're spending more than you're bringing in.

The X-axis shows Year and the Y-axis shows Amount ($).

Reading the Legends

Two legends appear on the chart:

Legend Position What It Shows
Upper area (green items) Income categories — Social Security, account distributions, salary, capital gains, etc.
Lower area (red items) Expense categories — housing, general expenses, Medicare, education, taxes, etc.

Each category gets its own color shade or hatch pattern so you can distinguish them within the stacked bars.

Example 1: Transitioning from Work to Retirement

A scenario showing someone retiring in 2029, with salary income tapering off and retirement distributions taking over.

What to notice:

  • 2025–2028: Tall green bars include SalaryAndBonus income. The large inflows from employment dwarf the retirement income that follows.
  • 2026–2027: The green bars spike because salary, Social Security, and distributions overlap during the transition years.
  • 2029 onward: Salary disappears. Income settles into a steady combination of Social Security and TaxFreeDistribution.
  • Red bars (expenses): Notice how Education costs appear in the early years then drop off. Housing and general expenses remain steady throughout.
  • The black net flow line: It's high during working years, dips during the transition, then stabilizes around $20,000–$30,000 positive — meaning this plan generally has surplus cash each year in retirement.
  • Income taxes: The hatched red segments show federal taxes, which are larger during working years and shrink in retirement as taxable income decreases.

Example 2: Fully Retired

A scenario covering roughly 35 years of retirement, with income from distributions and Social Security.

What to notice:

  • Income (green bars): This retiree draws from a mix of TaxableDistribution, TaxFreeDistribution, and SocialSecurity. The proportions shift over time as account balances change.
  • Expenses (red bars): Expenses remain relatively flat, dominated by general Expenses and Housing costs, with Medicare appearing as a separate segment.
  • The net flow line: It's positive through the mid-2040s (surplus years), then trends negative in later years as expenses outpace shrinking account distributions. This is a signal to investigate — are account balances running low?
  • Late years (2050s): The sharp drop in the net flow line and larger negative red bars suggest this plan may face cash flow pressure in later years. This is exactly the kind of insight the chart helps you spot.

How to Use This Chart

Quick health check

Look at the black net flow line. If it stays above zero for most years, your plan is generating more income than it spends. If it dips below zero, those are years where you'll need to draw down savings to cover the gap.

Spot transitions

The chart clearly shows when major income or expense changes happen: retirement date, Social Security start, mortgage payoff, education expenses ending, Medicare kicking in, or RMDs beginning.

Compare income sources

The stacked green bars show how your income composition changes over time. Early retirement might rely heavily on account distributions, while later years lean more on Social Security. You can see the balance at a glance.

Identify expense pressure

If the red bars grow taller over time while green bars shrink, your plan may face sustainability challenges. Look at which expense categories are growing — is it Medicare premiums increasing with age? Housing costs? General expenses?

Run "what if" scenarios

Try changing your inputs (retirement age, spending levels, Social Security start date) and compare how the chart changes. The shape of the bars and the net flow line will show you the impact immediately.

Keep in mind: The chart shows annual totals. Monthly timing within a year (when Social Security payments arrive vs. when bills are due) is not visible here, but is handled by the underlying month-by-month simulation engine.
