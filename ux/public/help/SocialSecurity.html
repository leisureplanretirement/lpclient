How Social Security Works in the Retirement Calculator

Setting Up Your Benefit

Tell the calculator your monthly Social Security benefit and when it starts. You can specify any start date — for example, if you are deferring to age 70 to maximise your benefit.

One benefit per person. The calculator supports one Social Security income stream per investor. If you and a spouse both have benefits, model them separately.

How the Benefit Grows Over Time

Each year your Social Security benefit increases by the Social Security COLA rate (default 2.5%). This matches how the real-world Cost-of-Living Adjustment works. You can change the assumed COLA rate to model different scenarios.

Scenario Sample Chat Prompt
Start benefit at age 67 "I will receive $2,400/month in Social Security starting at age 67."
Defer to age 70 "My Social Security benefit is $3,200/month starting at age 70."
Change the COLA assumption "Set the Social Security COLA rate to 2%."

How Much of Your Benefit Is Taxable

The IRS taxes a portion of Social Security benefits depending on your provisional income — a measure of combined income that includes half of your annual SS benefit plus everything else (IRA withdrawals, interest, wages, etc.).

Provisional income (single filer) Provisional income (married filing jointly) Taxable portion of SS
Below $25,000 Below $32,000 0% — SS is tax-free
$25,000 – $34,000 $32,000 – $44,000 Up to 50%
Above $34,000 Above $44,000 Up to 85%

Provisional income = ½ × annual SS benefit + all other income (IRA/401k withdrawals, interest, dividends, wages)

IRA withdrawals push SS into higher taxation. If you need to withdraw from a tax-deferred account (IRA, 401k) to cover expenses, those withdrawals count as income and can push your provisional income above the thresholds — causing more of your SS benefit to become taxable.

Practical example

You receive $24,000/year in Social Security and withdraw $20,000 from an IRA. Your provisional income is:

½ × $24,000 + $20,000 = $32,000

That puts a single filer just below the 85% threshold ($34,000), so up to 50% of the SS benefit may be taxable.

What Happens to Surplus SS Income

If your Social Security income exceeds your monthly expenses and Medicare premiums, the surplus is deposited into your savings account each month. It is not swept into your IRA or Roth — only the regular savings (brokerage) account receives the surplus.

Medicare Is Applied Automatically

Once you reach age 65 (or your specified Medicare start date), the calculator automatically adds Medicare Part B premiums, Part D, and any applicable IRMAA surcharges as monthly expenses. These reduce the effective surplus from Social Security more than simple arithmetic suggests.

IRMAA note: Medicare Part B and Part D premiums increase for higher-income retirees. The surcharge is based on your income from two years prior. If your income was high in the years before retirement, you may face higher premiums in the early years of the simulation.

Common Questions

Why is my SS partially taxable even with low expenses?

If you have savings or investment accounts, the market returns on those accounts count as income for Medicare IRMAA purposes but not for SS taxation (unrealised gains are not income). However, if you withdraw from an IRA, that withdrawal counts toward provisional income. Check whether the calculator is modelling IRA withdrawals you may not have intended.

Why does the surplus deposited to savings seem smaller than expected?

Medicare premiums are automatically deducted each month, and federal income tax on the taxable portion of your SS benefit is withheld at year-end. Both reduce the amount available to deposit to savings.
